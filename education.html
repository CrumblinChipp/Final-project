Educational Background

Grade School (2008-2014)

It's all a blur now, random blotches of memories from my grade school days. I wanted to believe I was a very smart child, almost an adult due to my younger sibling. Little did I know that adulthood wasn't just about screaming and crying children, but also about screaming and crying adults.

I attended two elementary schools: Ambulong Elementary School in my hometown and Batangas City South Elementary School, which was closer to my father's workplace. I transferred in the second grade. The change didn't affect me much, as we stayed in Ambulong for the first year before moving to a rented lot near my father's work. I mentioned earlier that I thought I was a smart kid, and this was reinforced when I transferred to South Elementary and was ranked first in my class for my first two years, second grade and third grade. I caught on quickly, memorized lines easily, and understood them well enough to explain them back in a clear manner. With all this praise, I developed a bit of a big head, after all, being favored by teachers has a significant impact on a child. In the fourth grade, I was transferred to the science class, the separate class for the "smart kids" as it was called. With my nonchalant attitude and big head, fueled by the attention of my previous teachers, I believed I could ace the class without trying, a mistake I realized quite early on but never acted on.

I had my first traumatic experience, well, at least for a child, when I was in the fourth grade. It was the first time my parents were called to school for my bad behavior, other than the time I wet my pants. I was humiliated in front of the class, and it has scarred me to this day. I can even remember the words my teacher said to me that day.

But time passes, and the present becomes the past. I continued to do the bare minimum, just enough to get by. In the sixth grade, I was removed from the science class. It hit me hard when I saw the list of students and my name wasn't there. I didn't even expect myself to be so affected. But the school year went on. Interestingly, the number of sixth-grade students was so small that there were only 20 students per class and only four sections, and the number of students kept decreasing, which surprisingly affected me a lot. For the first quarter of the school year, I was transferred to multiple sections. I had to introduce myself again and again as I was moved to another section. Some transfers lasted just a morning, and others a week, making it difficult for me to socialize. That is until I was retransferred to the science class again. This is when the world started to make sense in a way, realizing that the world doesn't revolve around me and it's not all sunshine and rainbows.

Junior High School (2014-2018)

I attended Batangas National High School, more commonly known as BANAHIS. It was during this time that I began to experience the common adolescent desire to belong.

In my eagerness to fit in, I became a people-pleaser, saying yes to everything, even at the expense of my own health and well-being. Fortunately, most of those who took advantage of my generosity were not malicious. It was also during this time that I discovered my passion for learning and exploring topics beyond what was taught in school or on television. The internet became my gateway to a vast world of knowledge.

My family had an unspoken rule that children could only have a phone after finishing high school. This rule had worked well for my two older siblings, as technology was not as prevalent during their time. They were able to navigate their school years without relying heavily on their phones, using them primarily for communication. However, I was an exception. I was given a tablet when I was in eighth grade. No one questioned my mother's decision to give me the tablet.

I was the first in my family to have access to the internet at an early age, so I took great care of my tablet. However, it was cracked before I even finished eighth grade, rendering it unusable. It was broken by one of my classmates, but I never identified the culprit. I panicked over how to explain the broken tablet to my parents. I hid the damaged device in my dresser, hoping my parents would not find it. My attempt at concealment was unsuccessful, as my younger sister discovered the broken tablet and promptly reported it to our parents. As expected, my parents were furious.

My brief access to the internet was cut short due to the tablet's destruction, leaving me relatively uninformed until I finished junior high school.

Senior High School (2018-2020)

I attended STI College Batangas for my senior high school years. This was where I found my true friends, friends I can still count on to this day.

Taking the STEM course, I was certain that I would be pursuing an engineering course in college, but I was still unsure about which specific field. I hadn't embarked on a self-discovery journey yet. Still carrying my nonchalant personality, I had the chance to achieve better grades along with my classmates, but I didn't take it. There was no particular reason; I just didn't see the point in doing so. It was also during this time that I and my group were introduced to a form of worship. Letting out emotions and hearing others' stories about their hardships turned out not to be my thing. I still joined them for these activities because I enjoyed their company, but my mind was always elsewhere.

I became a part of the school's student council, which was not my forte at all. I first became the class president. It turned out that being able to silence the rowdy boys in class gave me an immediate advantage for the position. For the student council elections, I didn't win the spot representing the STEM course, which I accepted wholeheartedly. The workload was already too much as we were graduating, and my classmates kept electing me for every position, which only embarrassed me because I kept losing. A few days later, on the day when the candidates were campaigning around campus, I was pulled out of class and told to run for a position. When I asked why, they said the original candidate was transferring schools and I was the next best thing. It was a roundabout way of saying I was their spare. So, I had a couple of hours to learn the campaign jingle, their names, and their projects before they went around campus that same day. On the day of the election, I was already prepared to lose. On the day of campaigning, I was a mess, constantly getting their slogan wrong. But it turned out that I was a hit with the 11th graders, and they voted for me in a landslide. Forced to take on the position, I tried my best to do what I could with a mental note to never do this again in college.

College (2020-present)

Even before stepping foot on campus, a series of events had already unfolded that would shape my college experience, beginning with the admissions process.

Having grown close to my high school friends, I made a decision that would ultimately cost me four years of pursuing my true passions.

On admissions day, I joined the hopeful throng of students vying for a spot at BSU. My classmates and I discussed our preferred programs, and the majority, influenced by their choices, opted for civil engineering. I, too, fell into this pattern, reasoning that choosing the same program would increase our chances of staying together and avoid the dreaded separation.

Initially, my program preferences were computer engineering, followed by mechanical engineering, and chemical engineering as a fallback. Programming had always fascinated me, and I was intrigued by the inner workings of codes. However, unfortunately, my curiosity wasn't strong enough to override my fear of being separated from my friends.

When my turn to register arrived, I altered my program lineup to civil, computer, and chemical engineering, not too far from my original aspirations.

As confirmation letters rolled out, I discovered that I was the only one in my friend group to have secured a spot in civil engineering. Due to my lack of awareness, I didn't even attempt to shift programs before classes began.

Then came the pandemic.

For someone who already struggled to socialize in the traditional school setting, online classes proved to be detrimental to my social life. The absence of peers my age and the lack of external interaction pushed me further into introversion and lowered my self-esteem. These were undoubtedly some of the most challenging years of my life.
